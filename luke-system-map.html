Conditions in Conflict.

Luke does not have one disease. He has four active systems — cardiac, renal, hepatic, and musculoskeletal — each with its own trajectory, and every treatment for one system creates tension in another. This document maps those interactions.

System 01 · Primary driver

❤ Cardiac

Presumptive cardiac disease, Lasix-responsive. 300 mg/day furosemide (8.4 mg/kg/day) without echocardiographic confirmation, without adjunctive cardiac medications (pimobendan, ACE-I, spironolactone). Echocardiography not accessible due to cost. NT-proBNP and troponin not yet drawn.

System 02 · Primary constraint

⬡ Renal

IRIS Stage 2 CKD. Creatinine 1.0→1.2→1.8 over twelve months. BUN volatile (43→35). Phosphorus 5.1 — above IRIS target of <4.6. SDMA 13, near ceiling. Both furosemide and carprofen are nephrotoxic. Urinalysis overdue since Sep 2023.

System 03 · Active concern

◎ Hepatic

Mild hepatocellular changes. ALT 46→121→128 (trending up). Bilirubin spiked to 1.4, resolved to 0.1. Lipase spiked to 1,382, resolved to 110. Carprofen (chronic NSAID) is the most likely driver. Imaging deferred for QoL.

System 04 · Managed — watch interactions

⊕ Musculoskeletal

Chronic hindlimb OA, multi-modal protocol. Carprofen 225 mg/day + amantadine 100 mg/day + Librela 20 mg as needed. Librela response is good and documented. Pain control is the quality-of-life anchor for Luke — reducing it has direct daily consequences. But carprofen is simultaneously the drug most stressing both the kidneys and the liver.

Core Tension 1
Cardiac↔Renal

Furosemide is essential for the heart. Furosemide is nephrotoxic. Reducing furosemide risks respiratory crisis; continuing it accelerates CKD. The only resolution is adding adjunctive cardiac medications that allow a lower diuretic dose — which requires an NT-proBNP or biomarker to justify.

Core Tension 2
Pain Control↔Kidneys + Liver

Carprofen controls pain that is real and daily. Carprofen is an NSAID with known nephrotoxic and hepatotoxic risk. The pain is visible; the organ damage is invisible until it isn't. Librela and amantadine partially substitute — the question is whether they can do enough to allow carprofen reduction.

System 01

Cardiac — The Organizing Fact

Everything in Luke's care plan is arranged around the cardiac disease. It is also the least confirmed diagnosis in the record.

What is known
  • 300 mg/day furosemide — dose not safely reducible without a plan
  • Lasix-responsive: respiratory effort improves markedly on the drug
  • May 2025 acute event: increased respiratory rate, lethargy, 98 lb weight (likely fluid)
  • Dr. Denney: "discontinuing Lasix is considered high-risk for a life-threatening respiratory event"
  • Dose at 8.4 mg/kg/day — at ACVIM Stage D threshold
  • Chloride 106 L on Sep '25 panel — furosemide-related electrolyte loss confirmed
What is not known
  • No echocardiogram — echocardiography not accessible due to cost
  • No NT-proBNP or troponin ever drawn
  • Disease type (DCM vs. MVD vs. other) unknown
  • Whether pimobendan, ACE-I, or spironolactone are indicated
  • Taurine status (diet history overlaps FDA DCM investigation window)
What would change the picture
  • NT-proBNP: quantifies cardiac wall stress without echo; can be drawn at Thornwood
  • Troponin I: baseline for any future acute event comparison
  • Taurine: single measurement rules in or out diet-associated DCM (reversible)
  • Adjunctive cardiac medications (pimobendan, ACE-I) could reduce furosemide requirement
  • Resting sleeping respiratory rate baseline established at home (<30 = compensated)

Why the cardiac diagnosis matters most. Luke's cardiac disease is the only one of his four active conditions that can immediately kill him — a respiratory crisis from furosemide discontinuation is a same-day event. The renal disease, hepatic changes, and musculoskeletal pain are all serious, but they operate on a weeks-to-months timescale. This asymmetry means cardiac management appropriately dominates all other decisions. Every other system is being treated within the constraints the cardiac disease sets.

⚠ Named Gap — Adjunctive Cardiac Medications

Standard ACVIM-aligned care for a dog at Luke's furosemide dose would include pimobendan (improves contractility so less diuretic is needed), an ACE inhibitor (reduces preload/afterload), and potentially spironolactone (supplemental diuretic allowing furosemide reduction). Luke has none of these. This is not a criticism of his care team — it may be a deliberate decision given his CKD (ACE inhibitors require monitoring in Stage 2 CKD) and his quality-of-life focus. But it is a gap worth naming explicitly to any new provider.

Home monitoring protocol. The resting sleeping respiratory rate (RSRR) is the most accessible, high-value home metric for cardiac decompensation in a Lasix-managed dog. The method: count breaths per minute while Luke is fully asleep (chest rising and falling), not during REM movements. Target below 30/min. A sustained reading above 30 — especially in the setting of reduced food interest or reduced carrot requests — should prompt same-day veterinary contact.

Where the Systems Fight

Six Active Conflicts

Each of these is a clinical decision that has no clean answer — managing one system requires accepting cost in another. A new provider needs to know all six are live before adjusting anything. Each card ends with a resolution path.

Cardiac ↔ Renal

The furosemide bind

Furosemide is essential for cardiac management and cannot be stopped. It is also nephrotoxic, accelerating CKD progression with every dose. The dose is both the treatment and the diagnostic marker — the heart's condition is inferred from how much furosemide it takes to keep Luke comfortable.

Resolution path: NT-proBNP to characterize cardiac status; adjunctive cardiac medications (pimobendan, ACE-I) to reduce furosemide requirement; urinalysis + UPC to substage CKD and determine whether ACE-I is indicated.
Pain Control ↔ Renal + Hepatic

The carprofen dilemma

Carprofen manages real, daily pain. It is also an NSAID with confirmed nephrotoxic and hepatotoxic effects. ALT has trended from 46 to 128 over three years. Furosemide + carprofen together stress the kidneys from two different mechanisms simultaneously. Librela and amantadine partially substitute but may not fully cover OA pain.

Resolution path: Trial gabapentin (renal dose adjustment needed); consider carprofen reduction to 150 mg/day if multi-modal coverage is adequate; high-dose omega-3 provides anti-inflammatory support.
Nutrition ↔ Renal + Cardiac

The phosphorus and omega-3 gaps

Luke's serum phosphorus is above the IRIS target and the dietary phosphorus content of the current food is unknown. His omega-3 intake is 6% of the cardiac-indicated dose. Both are nutritional levers that are addressable without a clinic visit.

Resolution path: Obtain food phosphorus content; high-dose fish oil supplementation to cardiac dose; consider phosphorus binder if food phosphorus is elevated.
Cardiac Cachexia ↔ Protein Restriction

Weight loss and muscle wasting

Luke has lost ~19 lb over 10 months (98→79.1 lb). Some is appropriate (fluid off diuresis) but cardiac cachexia is a genuine concern. IRIS Stage 2 recommends moderate protein restriction — but excessive restriction in a cachectic patient accelerates muscle loss.

Resolution path: Omega-3 at cardiac doses is muscle-preserving (anti-cachexia effect). Monitor weight monthly. Current 4 patties/day (1,408 kcal) meets maintenance needs.
Cardiac Diagnosis ↔ Evidence

The empirical treatment problem

The cardiac diagnosis rests entirely on furosemide response. No imaging, no biomarkers, no disease type confirmation. This means adjunctive cardiac medications (pimobendan, ACE-I) cannot be justified by standard criteria without additional workup.

Resolution path: NT-proBNP and troponin I drawn at next Thornwood visit. These are blood tests — no echocardiography required. A significantly elevated NT-proBNP would justify adjunctive therapy.
QoL Focus ↔ Diagnostic Workup

The workup deferral pattern

Multiple high-value tests have been named and deferred: bile acid panel, imaging, urinalysis. These reflect a deliberate QoL-focused approach by Rikki and the Thornwood team. Any new provider should understand this context and not treat deferrals as oversight.

Resolution path: Urinalysis and UPC are low-burden (urine sample only) and highest clinical value. NT-proBNP is a blood draw. These two are the minimum workup for a new provider visit.

Quality of Life Framework

End-of-Life Context

This is not an imminent-decision document. It is a planning framework that Rikki has opened with the Thornwood team and that any new provider should understand exists. The end-of-life conversation was explicitly opened in the May 14, 2025 visit note.

Signals the household watches
  • Carrot enthusiasm: daily QoL signal — Rikki tracks appetite for carrots as a barometer, not a count. A noticeable shift in interest precedes decline by hours to days.
  • Resting respiratory rate: target <30 breaths/min while fully asleep. Not formally tracked yet — establishing a baseline is a named open item.
  • Willingness to engage: greets, follows Rikki, responds to routine cues.
What a crisis looks like
  • Increased respiratory rate & effort at rest (>30 breaths/min while sleeping)
  • Open-mouth breathing or blue-gray gums — emergency
  • Non-productive retching + distended abdomen — GDV emergency
  • Sudden collapse or severe lethargy
  • Refusal to eat for 24+ hours in a food-motivated dog
What Rikki needs from providers
  • An explicit framework: what the team is watching for; at what point the conversation changes from management to transition planning.
  • No surprises: she has Sam's death as context; she knows what an unprepared crisis looks like and does not want to repeat it.
  • Honesty about trajectory: she is capable of holding difficult information and prefers it to uncertainty.
