Cardiac system

Heart — The Organizing Constraint

Everything below sits downstream of the cardiac disease. The most impactful interventions here reduce Luke's dependence on furosemide — which would simultaneously help his kidneys, electrolytes, and hepatic load.

NT-proBNP + Cardiac Troponin I
Blood biomarkers · cardiac staging · no referral needed
High priority Test

What it would do

NT-proBNP quantifies cardiac wall stress — elevated in structural heart disease. Troponin I detects acute myocyte injury. Together they provide a baseline for tracking and guide whether adjunctive cardiac medications are indicated — without requiring echocardiography. Can be drawn at a routine blood panel at Thornwood. Neither has ever been measured in Luke's record.

Why this first

  • Echocardiography is not accessible due to cost — NT-proBNP is the practical alternative
  • A significantly elevated NT-proBNP would justify adjunctive cardiac medications (pimobendan, ACE-I) without echo
  • A normal result would still not rule out disease but provides useful baseline context
  • Can be added to any upcoming blood draw — no separate appointment needed
Pimobendan · ACE Inhibitor · Spironolactone
Adjunctive cardiac medications — currently absent
High priority Medication

What they would do

  • Pimobendan — positive inotrope; improves cardiac contractility so less diuretic force is needed to keep lungs clear. ACVIM standard-of-care at Luke's furosemide dose.
  • ACE inhibitor (enalapril, benazepril) — reduces cardiac preload/afterload; also renal-protective if UPC >0.5. May allow furosemide reduction.
  • Spironolactone — potassium-sparing supplemental diuretic; allows furosemide reduction while maintaining diuretic effect.

Constraints & context

  • Pimobendan requires echo confirmation of structural disease before prescribing (ACVIM guideline)
  • ACE inhibitors require UPC ratio and creatinine monitoring in Stage 2 CKD — feasible but requires baseline
  • Luke has none of these medications — a named gap in standard cardiac care
  • May not align with current QoL-focused care philosophy — discuss explicitly with Thornwood
Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA + DHA)
Supplement · cardiac + anti-cachexia
High priority Supplement

What it would do

High-dose omega-3 (EPA+DHA) has documented cardiac benefit in dogs: anti-arrhythmic, anti-inflammatory, and muscle-preserving. It is one of the few supplements with ACVIM endorsement for cardiac patients. It also attenuates cardiac cachexia — relevant given Luke's ongoing weight loss trend.

Current gap: 94%. Luke gets ~132 mg EPA+DHA/day from supplements. Cardiac-indicated dose is ~2,300 mg/day. He is at 6% of target.

How to close the gap

  • Add a dedicated high-potency fish oil supplement (e.g. Nordic Naturals Omega-3 Pet, Welactin)
  • Target: 40 mg EPA+DHA per kg body weight per day ≈ ~1,440 mg/day minimum for Luke at 35.9 kg
  • No significant drug interactions with current regimen
  • May have mild antiplatelet effect at very high doses — not clinically significant at recommended levels
  • Can be introduced immediately without diagnostic workup
Whole-Blood Taurine
Blood test · DCM screening
Medium Test

What it would do

Luke's diet history includes extended grain-free and novel-protein raw periods (Pork Blocks, Pork & Shellfish) overlapping the FDA's 2018–2019 DCM investigation window. Renal system · IRIS Stage 2

Kidneys — Slowing the Progression

Creatinine has risen from 1.0 to 1.8 in 12 months. The primary stressors are furosemide (unavoidable) and carprofen (potentially reducible). Every lever here either removes a nephrotoxic load or improves monitoring resolution.

Urinalysis + UPC Ratio
Urine test · overdue since Sep 2023
High priority Test

What it would do

Last urinalysis was September 2023 — 1+ protein, cloudy. The September 2025 panel did not include urine (sample not collected). UPC ratio substages IRIS CKD: if UPC >0.5, an ACE inhibitor is indicated to reduce further glomerular damage. If UPC >1.0, proteinuria is a primary concern. Proteinuria status is completely unknown for Luke right now. This is the single highest-yield, lowest-barrier test available.

Carprofen Dose Reduction
Medication adjustment · NSAID reduction
High priority Medication

What it would do

Carprofen (225 mg/day) is nephrotoxic and hepatotoxic. Reducing the dose removes the most reducible chronic stressor on both the kidneys and liver. Dr. Denney named possible dose reduction in September 2025. Librela and amantadine are both non-NSAID — they may provide sufficient pain coverage to allow a meaningful reduction.

Constraints & context

  • Must not be stopped abruptly — taper with owner monitoring of mobility and pain signals
  • Carrot count and willingness to walk are Rikki's primary pain indicators
  • Librela (as-needed) + amantadine are already on board as non-NSAID analgesic support
  • Do not reduce without a clear pain management plan in place
Blood Pressure Measurement
Monitoring · hypertension screening
Medium Test

What it would do

Hypertension is a CKD progression driver — it accelerates glomerular damage and is an IRIS substaging criterion. Blood pressure has never been formally assessed in Luke's record. IRIS recommends BP measurement at every CKD monitoring visit. If hypertension is present, treatment (amlodipine) may slow CKD progression and also reduce cardiac afterload.

Phosphorus Management
Diet + possible binder · IRIS target not met
Medium Diet

What it would do

Luke's phosphorus is 5.1 mg/dL — above the IRIS Stage 2 target of <4.6. Elevated phosphorus accelerates CKD progression. Dietary phosphorus restriction or a phosphorus binder can bring this within target. The phosphorus content of Luke's current food (Steve's Real Food Pork & Shellfish) is not documented in the record.

Options

  • Confirm food phosphorus content — contact Steve's Real Food for nutritional panel
  • Phosphorus-restricted prescription diet — Hill's k/d, Royal Canin Renal (significant diet change; requires owner buy-in)
  • Intestinal phosphorus binder — aluminum hydroxide, calcium carbonate, or lanthanum carbonate given with meals; binds dietary phosphorus before absorption
  • Binders are the least disruptive option if Luke's food is working well otherwise
Hepatic system

Liver — Reducing the Load

ALT has trended from 46 → 121 → 128 U/L over three years. Carprofen is the most likely driver. The bilirubin spike in March 2025 (1.4, resolved to 0.1) and the gallbladder concern from Dr. G have not been formally investigated.

Bile Acid Panel
Blood test · hepatic function
Medium Test

What it would do

ALT elevation indicates hepatocellular damage but not hepatic function. Bile acids (pre- and post-prandial) assess actual liver function — whether the liver is processing toxins appropriately. Dr. G named the gallbladder as a concern and imaging was deferred for QoL reasons; a bile acid panel is a non-invasive blood test that can flag functional compromise without imaging.

Carprofen Dose Reduction (hepatic benefit)
Medication adjustment
High priority Medication

What it would do

Same intervention as the renal reduction — noted here because carprofen is hepatically metabolized and is the most likely contributor to Luke's rising ALT. Reducing the dose benefits both the kidneys and the liver simultaneously. The ALT trend (46 → 121 → 128) argues for urgency on this front.

Musculoskeletal system · managed

Pain Control — Maintaining Quality of Life

Luke's OA pain is currently managed (Librela as-needed, amantadine, carprofen). The goal here is maintaining or improving pain control while reducing the NSAID burden on kidneys and liver.

Gabapentin
Adjunctive analgesic · neuropathic pain
Medium Medication

What it would do

Gabapentin addresses neuropathic and central sensitization components of chronic pain — distinct from the NSAID mechanism of carprofen and the NGF mechanism of Librela. Adding it could allow carprofen dose reduction while maintaining pain coverage. It was discussed at the August 2025 visit but not started due to concern about wobbly gait.

Constraints & context

  • Side effect: sedation and ataxia — start low (100 mg) and monitor gait
  • Renal dose adjustment needed in CKD — important given Luke's Stage 2 status
  • No hepatic metabolism — does not add to liver load
  • Revisit if carprofen reduction is pursued

Diet & Supplementation

Nutritional Levers

Several of Luke's most impactful gaps can be addressed through diet and supplementation without requiring new prescriptions. These are the lowest-barrier interventions available.

Supplement · immediate, no Rx needed
High priority Supplement

What it would do

Close the omega-3 gap. Current intake ≈ 132 mg EPA+DHA/day from existing supplements. Cardiac-indicated dose ≈ 2,300 mg/day (40 mg/kg). A single high-potency fish oil product added daily would close this gap at very low cost and with no significant drug interactions.

  • Target: 4–6 softgels or ~2 pumps of liquid daily for Luke's weight
Sodium-Conscious Food Selection
Diet · cardiac management
Medium Diet

What it would do

Sodium restriction is a standard recommendation for cardiac patients — excess sodium increases fluid retention and cardiac workload. The sodium content of Luke's current food (Steve's Real Food Pork & Shellfish Blocks) is not documented in his record. Confirming this and, if elevated, considering lower-sodium options would reduce cardiac preload. Note: extreme sodium restriction in a dog on furosemide can cause electrolyte instability — moderate restriction is the target, not elimination.

Caloric Density Review
Diet · weight / muscle maintenance
Medium Diet

What it would do

Luke has lost ~19 lb over 10 months (98 lb peak → 79.1 lb at last visit). Some of this was intentional (fluid off, appropriate weight loss) but the slope is steep and cardiac cachexia is a genuine concern. His daily caloric target was set when he weighed more — it may need recalibration. Confirming kcal/oz on the Pork & Shellfish label and calculating whether current portions meet maintenance needs is a low-cost starting point. The Wild Biome consult from 2024 set a 1,500 kcal/day target that may no longer apply at his current weight.

Monitoring protocols

What to Track at Home

These are not interventions but early-warning systems. Rikki is highly engaged and capable — these protocols give her clinical-grade signals without clinic visits.

Resting Sleeping Respiratory Rate (RSRR)
Home monitoring · cardiac decompensation
High priority Monitoring

What it would do

The RSRR is the most sensitive, accessible home signal for cardiac decompensation in a furosemide-managed dog. Count breaths per minute while Luke is fully asleep (chest rising and falling only — not during REM movements). Establish a baseline over several nights. A sustained reading above 30 breaths/min should prompt same-day veterinary contact. Not currently being tracked.

Protocol

  • Count for 30 seconds, multiply by 2 — or count for 60 seconds
  • Measure when fully asleep, not drowsy
  • Log for 7–10 nights to establish baseline
  • Free apps: Cardalis App (Ceva), Cardiosense
  • Threshold: >30 breaths/min consistently = same-day call to Thornwood
Carrot Count as QoL Signal
Home monitoring · already in use
Active Monitoring

Context

Rikki uses Luke's daily carrot requests as the primary quality-of-life metric. A drop in carrot count has preceded clinical decline by hours to days. This is a sophisticated and valid proxy for appetite, energy, and comfort. The clinical team should be aware of this metric and treat a reported "carrot count drop" as a meaningful clinical signal — not just an owner concern. No changes needed; this system is working.
