Helping Luke Thrive.

Nutrition, longevity, movement, bodywork and sensory ideas — every one weighed against Luke's cardiac and renal reality, and graded by how good the evidence actually is. Compiled by his owner as a starting point for the professionals who know him.

This is decision-support for conversation — not medical advice, and not a directive. Luke's mix of advanced (presumptive) heart failure + kidney disease makes him a genuinely fragile, individual case. Nothing here should be started, stopped, or dosed without his own veterinary team — ideally his vet plus a cardiologist, a board-certified veterinary nutritionist (DACVN), and a credentialed rehab therapist (CCRT/CCRP). Owner-compiled from published veterinary sources, as of July 2026.

Two red lines that filter everything below

  • Cardiac ceiling (confirmed). 300 mg furosemide ÷ 35.9 kg = 8.36 mg/kg/day — at or over the 2019 ACVIM threshold that defines Stage D ("refractory") heart failure. His diuresis is near-maximal and cardiac reserve is minimal. The two things that can tip him into acute pulmonary edema are over-exertion and any acute increase in fluid/preload.
  • Renal knife-edge. IRIS Stage 2 CKD on a high-dose diuretic means he lives between the kidney's need for hydration and the diuretic's push toward dehydration, with phosphorus already above target (5.1). This filters diet, supplements, fluids, heat, and travel.
  • The diagnoses are softer than they look. The heart disease is empiric (no echo/biomarkers); the kidney numbers were drawn on a high diuretic that itself distorts them; no urinalysis since 2023; Weimaraners are a DCM-predisposed breed with no taurine level ever checked. The highest-value "modern" move may be confirming the picture before optimizing around it.

0Step zero — confirm the picture

Before re-engineering anything, the most evidence-based step is characterizing what's actually being managed. All vet/specialist-led.

  • Echocardiogram + NT-proBNP Strong — confirm CHF and tell valve disease (MMVD) from dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM). Changes the whole plan; Weimaraners can get taurine-responsive DCM.
  • Whole-blood taurine level Moderate — cheap; if low, taurine-responsive DCM can partly reverse. Test before treating.
  • Repeat renal panel + phosphorus + a fresh urinalysis/UPC Moderate — on a clean, hydrated sample, to true up his IRIS stage before protein/phosphorus decisions (no UA since 2023).
  • Work up the rising ALT Moderate — carprofen itself can injure the liver; look before reaching for a liver supplement that could mask the signal.

1Nutrition & diet — the cardiac-vs-renal core

Cardiac diets restrict sodium and protect protein; renal diets restrict phosphorus and moderate protein. They collide on protein — and the ACVIM consensus lands in Luke's favor: avoid low-protein renal diets unless severe renal failure is present. He's Stage 2 (mild-moderate), so the default is do not severely protein-restrict him — protect his muscle (his ideal BCS 5/9 is an asset to hold). Moderate

How a nutritionist reconciles it for this dog:

  • Moderate high-quality protein (egg white / whey are low-phosphorus-per-gram) — not severe restriction.
  • Control phosphorus with an intestinal binder given with meals and titrated to blood phosphorus — the pivotal move that lets one diet serve both organs. Target Stage-2 phosphorus ≈ 2.7–4.6 mg/dL (his is 5.1). Moderate–strong This is the single strongest dietary lever in CKD.
  • Moderate — not extreme — sodium. Over-restriction activates hormonal systems that can worsen kidney perfusion. The easy owner win is a hidden-sodium audit: pill-pocket cheese/deli meat/peanut butter and treats can quietly dominate a cardiac dog's sodium.
  • Build in omega-3 (below) and high-moisture feeding (add water/low-sodium broth; multiple water stations).

Is the raw pork & shellfish diet a good fit? Honestly — no, for three reasons (with one merit).

  • Phosphorus & protein are unrestricted (raw meat + bone is high in both) — the opposite of what Stage 2 CKD needs. Moderate
  • Uncontrolled/added sodium (goat milk, shellfish, cured pork) — a cardiac liability.
  • Pathogen risk in a higher-risk host. Every major body (FDA, WSAVA, AAHA, AVMA) advises against raw meat diets; pork/shellfish add their own hazards. Luke is a senior on chronic carprofen (GI-vulnerable) with two organ diseases, plus household zoonotic risk. Strong
  • The one merit — high moisture — is real, and a cooked, reformulated custom diet keeps it while fixing the rest.

DCM / grain-free / taurine: his current diet is meat-based (not the pulse-heavy "BEG" pattern linked to diet-associated DCM), so food-related DCM risk is low — but because his heart diagnosis is unconfirmed in a DCM-prone breed, a taurine level is still worth it.

Who: this is a textbook board-certified veterinary nutritionist (DACVN) problem — no shelf diet fits (renal diets too low-protein for his heart; cardiac diets don't restrict phosphorus for his kidney). Directory: acvn.org.

2Omega-3 (EPA/DHA) — the clearest single win

The one nutrient with moderate evidence across all three of his problems, and it may cut his NSAID load:

  • Kidney Moderate — fish oil was renoprotective in the classic induced-CKD dog study (better GFR, less proteinuria) while omega-6 worsened injury.
  • Heart Moderate — reduces arrhythmia and cardiac cachexia, improves appetite/lean mass; ACVIM Class IIa, especially with muscle loss or arrhythmia.
  • Joints Moderate — the best-evidenced OA nutraceutical, with a trial showing a carprofen-sparing effect — directly relevant to his rising ALT and CKD.

The gap is real. Cardiac/renal target ≈ ~2,300 mg EPA+DHA/day for a 35.9 kg dog (joint indications higher; safe ceiling ~5,400 mg). His ~132 mg/day is ~6% of that — even a full dose sits well under the ceiling.

  • Source: purity/oxidation-tested fish oil from small cold-water fish. Krill/algal under-deliver EPA; flax is inadequate; avoid cod-liver oil (too much vitamin A/D — the D matters for his kidney).
  • Cautions: titrate up slowly (GI upset), count the ~70–110 kcal, pause high-dose ~1–2 weeks before any elective surgery/dental. Vet sets the exact dose and coordinates the carprofen-sparing opportunity.

3Nutraceuticals & "longevity" nutrients — graded honestly

  • SAM-e + silybin (Denamarin) Moderate — most relevant to his rising ALT (a controlled study lowered ALT). But diagnose why ALT is rising first.
  • Taurine / L-carnitine Strong if deficient else weak — big benefit only with documented deficiency. Test, don't assume; L-carnitine is renally cleared — don't megadose in CKD.
  • Green-lipped mussel Weak–moderate — fair optional joint adjunct; check the label for sodium/phosphorus.
  • CoQ10 (ubiquinol) Weak — raises blood levels but the best canine trial showed no clinical benefit. Safe, low priority.
  • Probiotic "kidney/gut" products (Azodyl-type) Weak–none — a controlled study found no effect on kidney values. Better renal levers are the diet + binder.
  • Keep his glucosamine/chondroitin/MSM (low concern) — but it's not a priority.

Read every "heart/kidney support" label for these four hidden ingredients

  • Vitamin D (cholecalciferol, cod-liver oil) → hypercalcemia / kidney harm.
  • Added phosphorus (bone meal, dicalcium phosphate, organ blends).
  • Blind potassium or magnesium — supplement only to a measured level.
  • Hidden sodium. Also skip megadose vitamin C (oxalate).

Of his meds, Librela is the "clean" one (no known supplement interactions); amantadine cautions against serotonergic supplements (5-HTP, St. John's Wort).

4Frontier longevity — a sober "not for Luke, not now"

The clearest answer comes from the researchers' own rules: the two most rigorous canine longevity trials (Dog Aging Project rapamycin; the Leap Years senolytic trial) both explicitly exclude dogs with significant heart, kidney, and liver disease. Luke is excluded on at least three counts. These are meant for relatively healthy seniors to slow aging — not to treat a fragile, multi-morbid patient.

  • Rapamycin Experimental · renal risk — the larger controlled dog trial was null for heart benefit; mTOR inhibitors can worsen proteinuria — a direct threat to his kidneys.
  • Senolytics (dasatinib) Contraindication-level — causes fluid retention / pleural effusion — dangerous in CHF.
  • NMN / NR (NAD+) Weak/unregulated — no independent canine proof; renally-cleared metabolites → caution in CKD.
  • Loyal (LOY-002) Pending — not yet available; a calorie-restriction-mimetic in a cachexia-prone CHF dog is an unquantified concern. Worth watching over 1–2 years as a future, healthier-day question — not for Luke now.

A longevity researcher's honest take: "This dog is exactly who we screen out. His prognosis is governed by his CHF and CKD, not the aging rate these target. Optimize heart, kidney, pain and quality of life instead."

5Physical therapy & rehab — honoring "the athlete," within the ceiling

Reframed honestly: for a Stage D heart, the kind athletic goal is movement under the heart's ceiling — preserving comfort, dignity and hindlimb function — a permanent "deload," never a peak. Rehab guidance is explicit that it's contraindicated when heart disease isn't well-compensated, and that cardiac/frail dogs work in 3–5 minute bouts (they can't signal distress).

  • PROM (passive range of motion) Low-risk — the therapist moves the joints; Luke does zero work → essentially no cardiac load. The lowest-risk hands-on modality; a therapist can teach you to do it gently at home.
  • Controlled short land exercise Weak–moderate — slow leash walks, cavaletti (low rails), sit-to-stand, weight-shifting; a 2026 study of exactly this improved hip motion and muscle. Best fit for his OA — flat ground, 3–5 min, frequent rests, stop on any breathing change. No fetch, hills, or heat.
  • Gentle balance/proprioception Weak — standing on foam/a disc; low demand, targets senior wobble; keep it short, calm, supervised.

The standout AVOID: swimming / hydrotherapy

Water immersion raises pressure that pushes blood into the chest, increasing cardiac preload — exactly the stimulus that can trigger pulmonary edema in a Stage D heart at maximal diuresis. Veterinary sources list heart disease as a hydrotherapy contraindication; swimming is the worst (full immersion + high exertion). If water work is ever raised, only shallow underwater-treadmill, only with cardiology clearance and monitoring — never swimming, never owner-driven. Mechanism strong

Who: a rehab vet / CCRT-CCRP working directly with his cardiologist, with cardiology clearance before any activity plan — not a DIY program.

6Stretching & massage / bodywork

  • Gentle massage / relaxation bodywork Weak, very low-risk — arguably the best fit on the whole list: soothing, eases the compensatory muscle tension arthritic dogs build, owner-deliverable, no cardiac load. Light strokes on thigh/shoulder muscle. Avoid pressing over the spine, bony points, the chest, and the kidney region (mid-back). Stop at any flinch.
  • Gentle stretching / PROM at home Weak — good for his hindlimbs; warm up with a couple minutes of calm hands-on (not a brisk walk or a heat pack over his trunk); short holds, never into pain. Best value: have a rehab therapist teach 3–4 stretches on his actual joints.
  • Consumer percussion massagers / massage guns No evidence — skip — his two off-limit zones (chest for CHF, mid-back for CKD) are exactly what you'd target. Hands do the same job with zero device risk.

7Sensory & haptic — the Woojer MAT he loves, and its cousins

Luke already lies on a Woojer MAT while music plays, and enjoys it — and that's a good thing. Important correction: the MAT is not the chest-strap vest and not a gaming gadget. It's a vibroacoustic wellness mat he lies on (75 × 30 in, six low-frequency transducers, 1–250 Hz), marketed by Woojer for relaxation, sleep, and stress relief. The vibration is distributed gently from below, and — the part that matters most — he chooses to get on and off it himself. A self-selected comfort he seeks out is exactly the kind of enrichment a later-chapter, quality-of-life plan wants. Weak evidence · low risk

Vibroacoustic therapy has a modest human evidence base for relaxation and calming the nervous system, and calming music has weak-to-moderate evidence for lowering stress in dogs. The haptic part is unproven in dogs, but at gentle levels he can leave at will, it's plausibly soothing rather than stressful — the opposite of the exertion/heat hazards elsewhere on this page. Sensible cautions, drawn from Woojer's own MAT manual + his heart and kidneys:

  • Keep the intensity/volume gentle. The manual itself advises a moderate level; relaxing settings, not heavy bass — which is how he already uses it. There's no reason to drive the low-frequency amplitude hard.
  • Watch his resting breathing on it — his cardiac gauge. Calm, easy breaths while he lies there = all good; any rise in rate or effort = time to get up. This turns a nice habit into a monitored-safe one.
  • Mind warmth. The manual warns the mat can get warm; Luke is heat-sensitive (CHF + diuretic, thin "blue" coat), so make sure it isn't warm under him and he can always move off to a cool spot.
  • Free choice, supervised — never confine him to it; let him self-select (he does).
  • Give his vet a heads-up. Woojer advises anyone with a "special medical condition" to check with a doctor; the honest analog is a quick mention to his vet/cardiologist — who will very likely be glad he has a calming ritual. (The mat contains magnets — only relevant if he ever had a pacemaker/implant, which he doesn't.)

Two related options with their own evidence, if you ever want something in addition to the mat:

  • PEMF (Assisi Loop) Moderate — best device option — a double-blind trial improved gait; non-thermal, non-exerting (doesn't trip his heat/exertion wires), possibly NSAID-sparing. Vet sets placement — over the sore limb/joint, off the chest and kidneys.
  • Pressure wrap / ThunderShirt Weak–mixed, low-risk — the studied version of "comforting sensory input" (smaller heart-rate rise in wrapped anxious dogs; no adverse effects). Fit snug-not-tight so it never restricts chest expansion; remove if breathing looks labored.
  • Photobiomodulation / therapeutic laser Moderate, mixed — he already tolerated it; Class IV lasers make real heat, so the operator must keep it moving and not build heat over the chest.
  • Therapeutic ultrasound Deprioritize — weak evidence, and its thermal mode is exactly the heat-over-the-trunk to avoid.

8Environment & quality of life — where much of the real value lives

  • Sleeping respiratory rate (SRR) — the single most useful home tool. Moderate–strong Count breaths while he sleeps, log 2–3×/week — daily around Montana. Establish his sea-level baseline now so an altitude-driven rise is detectable; a sustained climb toward/over ~30/min means call the vet (confirm his exact number).
  • Air quality — the biggest travel hazard. Strong Wildfire smoke raises heart-failure events within days and hits cardiac patients hardest. Watch AQI daily (AirNow.gov) — 101–150 = cut exertion; 151+ = indoors, potty-only. Run a true-HEPA purifier sized to his rest room; car windows up, AC on recirculate.
  • Altitude (~4,820 ft). Moderate Less oxygen = more cardiac work. Pre-travel cardiology exam; ask about gradual rest on arrival, whether portable oxygen should travel, and any monitoring/med adjustments. Smoke + altitude + heat stack — the most dangerous combination.
  • Orthopedic bedding Weak–moderate — thick solid-foam, waterproof-lined + washable (high-dose furosemide = frequent large urine output), floor-level (no stepping up).
  • Ramps + traction Rationale — a vehicle ramp (he's 79 lb) spares arthritic hindlimbs; non-slip runners / toe grips prevent slip-splay falls.
  • Walking Wheels cart In progress · Jul 2026 — a rear-support wheelchair (owner assembling all parts/straps) to let him walk at speed without loading the painful right rear leg; use for supervised outings only, unclip for rest. Toe drag noted — pair with paw protection. Owner's current-state notes are in the timeline and Our Time.
  • GDV vigilance Moderate — small, slow-fed meals 2–3×/day; no vigorous activity 1–2 hr around meals; know the signs (unproductive retching, distended abdomen, restlessness = surgical emergency); ask about gastropexy. Default to floor-level bowls (raised-bowl evidence is conflicting and one landmark study associated them with more GDV).
  • Warmth, done safely — his thin dilute "blue" coat chills easily and warmth eases stiff joints, but he's the classic burn-risk profile: pet-specific pad, lowest setting, towel barrier, warm only part of the bed, never in a warm car (heat + diuretic + CHF is a dangerous trio).
  • Travel logistics — constant water (never restrict water on a diuretic), frequent potty stops, waterproof pads, meds + written records + a destination 24-hr ER/cardiology contact identified before departure. Any dose/timing change is vet-only — ignore generic "give an extra furosemide" internet advice.
  • QoL tracking — a written weekly log using the HHHHHMM scale (simple) and ideally the cardiac-specific FETCH questionnaire, paired with the SRR log, gives the vet an objective early-warning trend instead of a gut feeling.

✕What to avoid, given his heart + kidneys

  • Swimming / full-immersion hydrotherapy, and any over-exertion — hills, fetch, hot-day / smoky-day / high-altitude activity.
  • Any single off-the-shelf disease diet — standard renal diets (cachexia risk) and unrestricted-phosphorus cardiac diets are both wrong alone.
  • Continuing the raw pork/shellfish diet as-is (unrestricted phosphorus, added sodium, pathogen risk in a senior NSAID dog).
  • Owner-initiated subcutaneous or IV fluids — routine in kidney-only dogs, but can trigger pulmonary edema in CHF. Vet-directed only.
  • Frontier longevity drugs — rapamycin, dasatinib senolytics, NMN/NR; Loyal isn't available and targets healthier seniors.
  • Supplements hiding vitamin D, added phosphorus, blind potassium/magnesium, or sodium; megadose vitamin C.
  • High-intensity vibration or heat concentrated over the chest/kidneys — massage guns, thermal ultrasound over the trunk, laser dwelling over the chest, or cranking any device to a strong setting. (His Woojer MAT at gentle levels, which he lies on by choice, is fine — see §7.)
  • Over-restricting sodium — the target is moderate, not maximal.
  • Raised food bowls by default; high-dose fish oil in the 1–2 weeks before elective surgery/dental.
