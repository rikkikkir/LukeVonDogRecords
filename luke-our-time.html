Our TimeLuke & Rikki · on a 25‑hour day

Luke and I live on a 25‑hour day that shifts by 84 minutes each day. This accommodation has been necessary due to my genetic condition — a roughly 25‑hour circadian rhythm. Finding that invisible thread through time was difficult, and finding repeatability inside that time has been tricky.

Where Luke is right now

Luke is still very active, but he's steadily slowed over the past two years — from pain, not lost enthusiasm. His back right leg is the sore one (both are sore, the right more so): he rises slowly, increasingly runs with both back legs moving as one, and on a startled quick turn the leg can give out — he's fallen. He's changed how he settles, too — he spins to swing his right leg under him and rest on the hip rather than bend the joint deeply, and sits with that leg off to the side.

He digs one arm at a time now, and much slower — rather than the fast back-and-forth digging he used to do, like he was swimming. He gets winded faster, needing more breaks. I occasionally hear a toenail drag on a step. I want to slow and reverse his decline as much as I can.

As of July 2026, I'm getting him set up with a Walking Wheels cart — I now have all the parts, pieces, and straps. He'll enjoy the walks at speed, without the pain in his knee, hip, and ligaments.

Our time together

Since August 2025, Luke and I have been living on a cadence of wake, breakfast, snack, snack, dinner, bedtime. Regardless of the time we wake, he and I use a 24‑hour clock to follow a predictable path through time. Luke knows the timing, the cadence, and can read the clock. Luke does not have a circadian rhythm and lives in the same time bubble I do.

He's given me safe passages through the nights, and companionship at every hour.

I am eternally grateful to have gotten to spend this time with him. He and I have lived so many beautiful nights of solitude, and I am glad to now get to show him the places he's seen in my dreams — the town, mountains, and family I often have visualized and hold in my mind. Luke can sense and feel those energetic waves, and he has responded with amazement and delight every time a familiar vision comes into reality.

I want him to live a healthy, strong, happy life — as long as is kind.

— Rikki, in her own words (lightly corrected for typos only)

What "Non‑24" means

A Non‑24‑hour sleep‑wake rhythm (the free‑running type) means the body clock isn't held to 24 hours. Rikki's runs about 25.4 hours — roughly 84 minutes longer than the clock each day — so her wake time slides about 84 minutes later every day and loops through the whole 24‑hour clock about every 17 days. It's genetic, not a habit or a choice. The two charts below show a single month of it: the clock drifts, but the cadence holds.

How our day runs

Wake→ Breakfast→ Snack→ Snack→ Dinner→ Bedtime

Luke's medications and meals ride this cadence, not the clock. Whatever hour the day begins, the order and spacing stay the same — this is the predictability that keeps him steady.

Breakfast is his "breakfast medicine" — all of his supplements and medications, including the first 3 tablets of furosemide (Lasix, 150 mg). Dinner is his "dinner medicine" — the second 3 tablets of furosemide (150 mg). His meals run about 3 hours apart, so the two Lasix doses fall at breakfast and dinner (~9 hours apart across his day). "AM / PM" on the med sheet means relative to when the day begins — any hour of the clock — not 8 a.m. and 8 p.m.
