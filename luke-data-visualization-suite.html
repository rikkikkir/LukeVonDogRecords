What this measures

Furosemide is a loop diuretic: it forces the kidneys to excrete sodium and water, reducing fluid load on the heart. The dose trajectory from 50→100→300 mg/day reflects progressive cardiac disease requiring more diuretic force to keep Luke's lungs clear. The dose is both the treatment and the diagnostic marker — the heart's condition is inferred from how much furosemide it takes to keep Luke comfortable.

How it is impacting Luke

At 8.4 mg/kg/day, Luke is at the ACVIM threshold for Stage D refractory CHF — but without the adjunctive cardiac medications (pimobendan, ACE inhibitor, spironolactone) that stage D patients typically also receive. This means furosemide is doing all of the cardiac work alone, and its renal cost (chloride wasting, volume depletion) is unmitigated by cardiac offloading agents. The dose cannot safely be reduced without either an echo-guided plan or adjunctive medications.

How to adjust the furosemide burden
Reducing the dose (requires specialist plan)
  • Pimobendan — positive inotrope; improves contractility so less diuretic force is needed. Standard-of-care addition for dogs with confirmed structural disease.
  • ACE inhibitor (enalapril, benazepril) — reduces cardiac preload and afterload; may allow lower furosemide. Standard ACVIM recommendation.
  • Spironolactone — potassium-sparing diuretic; supplemental effect allowing furosemide reduction.
Monitoring dose adequacy
  • Resting sleeping respiratory rate (<30 breaths/min is target — not currently tracked at home)
  • Weight stability (sudden gain = fluid re-accumulation)
  • Owner observation of breathing effort and energy level
  • NT-proBNP (cardiac biomarker) if echo is not pursued
Cannot do
  • Do not stop or reduce furosemide without a veterinary plan — Dr. Denney has named this as high risk for life-threatening respiratory event
  • All dose changes require monitoring Luke's breathing response within 24–48 hours

What this measures

Creatinine is a muscle breakdown waste product filtered by the kidneys. When creatinine rises, it signals that the kidneys are filtering less efficiently — either because nephrons have been lost, blood flow is reduced, or there is an acute stress on the kidneys. IRIS Stage 2 (1.4–2.8 in dogs) carries actionable recommendations: dietary phosphorus restriction, avoid nephrotoxic drugs where possible, hydration support. What this measures

BUN reflects the kidneys' ability to clear urea, a protein breakdown product. Unlike creatinine, BUN is highly influenced by protein intake, hydration status, and GI bleeding. A dog eating more protein or that is mildly dehydrated will have higher BUN even if kidney function is unchanged. This makes BUN useful but not definitive — it always needs to be interpreted alongside creatinine and hydration context.

How it is impacting Luke

BUN rose from 19 to 43 across 2024–2025, then improved to 35 at the March 2026 draw. The Comprehensive Health Overview framed this as "volatile rather than steadily worsening" — an important note, but 35 is still above the reference of 28. The volatility itself is informative: furosemide, dietary changes, and hydration shifts can all swing BUN without reflecting true nephron loss.

How to move BUN

  • Moderate dietary protein reduction (IRIS Stage 2 guidance)
  • Avoid protein-dense treats in excess
  • Treat any concurrent GI bleeding source (would elevate BUN)

What this measures

ALT is an intracellular liver enzyme that leaks into the bloodstream when hepatocytes (liver cells) are damaged or stressed. Elevated ALT does not diagnose a specific liver disease — it indicates hepatocellular injury and warrants investigation of the cause. In dogs on long-term NSAIDs like carprofen, a rising ALT trend is a recognized adverse effect requiring monitoring and possible dose adjustment.

How it is impacting Luke

The 46 → 121 → 128 trend over twelve months is consistent with cumulative hepatocellular stress. At 128, Luke is just above the reference ceiling of 121. Dr. G's email (Mar '25) named "early changes to his gallbladder and potentially his liver" in response to the bilirubin spike at that same draw. Carprofen is the most likely contributor — it is the drug with the clearest hepatotoxic risk in this protocol.

How to protect the liver and lower ALT
Primary lever: reduce carprofen
  • Dr. Denney explicitly named carprofen dose reduction as an option (Sep '25 email)
  • Librela (anti-NGF) + amantadine (NMDA antagonist) may provide sufficient pain coverage at a lower NSAID dose
  • Reducing from 225 mg/day to 150 mg/day (2 tabs) would reduce both hepatic and renal load
  • Gabapentin (discussed but not started) could also partially substitute
Hepatoprotective supplements
  • SAMe (S-adenosylmethionine) — hepatoprotective; well-studied in dogs on long-term NSAIDs. Nutramax Denosyl is the veterinary product.
